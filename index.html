Entrepreneurs Only

Hacker News launched in February 2007 as Startup News. It began as a social news site catered specifically to programmers and entrepreneurs.

The site was created and initially moderated by Paul Graham, co-founder of Y Combinator, a seed accelerator that invests mostly in tech startups. Y Combinator offers funding, advice and a wealth of networking opportunities for startups who pass a vigorous entrance application and interview.

Hacker News was created with an experimental programming language, Arc, to fill the void left by Reddit's increasing popularity. In its infancy, Reddit was funded and guided by Y Combinator, resulting in many of its early users being connected to the company. Reddit's increasing popularity, however, meant that content shifted from a concentration of startup-related links to images, videos and political news.

Because it is so tightly coupled with the startup world, many of HN's discussions focus on digital entrepreneurship in some form or another. The site itself was created to help Y Combinator get to know potential founders. In fact, being an active member on the site is a requirement to apply to Y Combinator.

Identity of a Hacker

Merriam-Webster, and likely many lay folk, define a hacker primarily as someone who secretly gains access to computer systems to steal information, cause damage or wreak similarly destructive havoc. However, this definition doesn't quite fit with the content of Hacker News. The site isn't designed for malevolent technical braggadocio. Rather, the original definition of a hacker in the context of computers is intended. The definition created and embodied by the AI hackers of MIT, the hardware hackers of Silicon Valley and the video game hackers of the 80s.

Steven Levy's book (which I presented on here), "Hackers - Heroes of the Computer Revolution" delves deeply into these generations of hackers But, before doing so, it defines precisely what a hacker is: one who promotes access, freedom, decentralization and learning through computers. Driven by the Hacker Ethic, a hacker pursues information and knowledge and seeks to set it free.

Many posts on Hacker News echo this sentiment. From security post-mortems intended to educate the community, to cheat sheets for common software. Users of Hacker News aren't the nefarious, malicious beings propagated by the current definition of the word. Instead, they are merely voraciously curious technophiles.

#noemail

Although perhaps not as vehemently anti-email as Paul Jones, HN's creation was partly caused by Paul Graham's dissatisfaction with the technology.

In a blog post announcing the creation of the site, he cites the difficulty in communication caused by email.

"Yesterday we launched Startup News, a new component of our site with a user-ranked list of startup-related links. We created this partly for our own use: we've now funded about a hundred people, so it doesn't work well anymore to send links around by email."
 Paul Graham

Many posts on Hacker News focus on showing off new, potentially disruptive technologies. And, although email's replacement hasn't quite been settled on, I wouldn't be surprised if it was initially posted on HN. In fact, in a later blog post, Paul Graham explains that the disruption of email is one startup idea that Y Combinator sees often, stating:

"Email was not designed to be used the way we use it now. Email is not a messaging protocol. It's a todo list. Or rather, my inbox is a todo list, and email is the way things get onto it. But it is a disastrously bad todo list."
 Paul Graham

Cognitive Surplus

Hacker News itself is a product of cognitive surplus. The programming language and platform that it stands upon was created by Paul Graham. Graham created Arc, a dialect of Lisp as a personal project in 2001. And, it would be with Arc that he would develop and release Hacker News 6 years later.

Clay Shirky, would likely agree with my classification of HN as a positive result of cognitive surplus. Unlike other frivolous pursuits (like LOLCats), Hacker News is a product of free time that allows its users new forms of collaboration. While the concept of HN might not be entirely novel (link aggregators are quite old), the content and discussions fostered on the site are.

Two common threads in particular showcase these new forms of collaboration. Ask HN, where users ask the community for technical or personal help and Show HN, where users show off apps/games/sites/products they've developed. So, not only is the platform itself the result of cognitive surplus, but much of the content is as well.

The Shallows

On the opposing end of Shirky's views is Nicholas Carr. Carr refutes the idea that time spent on the Internet can result in a net positive for users. Instead, he states that the Internet might have detrimental effects on cognition that can diminish the capacity for concentration and deep contemplation.

Rather than being a place that fosters new types of collaboration and enjoys the technologies birthed from this collaboration, Carr's Internet has negative cognitive effects on its users. Its ever-malleable nature, quickly moving pop culture and immense velocity of information have resulted in smaller attention spans and an inability to think and consider deeply. Rather than benefiting from cognitive surplus, users are inundated with cognitive overload.

Hacker News has editors and moderators who seek to prevent the site from aiding Carr's argument. As part of their job, they ensure that posts on the site are mostly productive and that discussions don't heavily stray off topic. However, Paul Graham himself recognizes that social applications can be addictive.

"I've learned a lot from things I've read on HN. I've written several essays that began as comments there. So I wouldn't want the site to go away. But I would like to be sure it's not a net drag on productivity. What a disaster that would be, to attract thousands of smart people to a site that caused them to waste lots of time. I wish I could be 100% sure that's not a description of HN."
 Paul Graham

The Future Internet

We viewed Tim O'Reilly's State of the Valley talk given in 2013. Throughout the talk, O'Reilly predicts the direction the internet will go in the coming years and posits that the Internet will eventually grow into a "Global Brain". This Global Brain relies heavily on collaboration between multiple entities in order to grow and sustain.

The Internet itself brought about a new era of collaboration. Geographic boundaries become smaller hurdles. Sites like Hacker News, those that foster discussion, place an emphasis on this collaboration. For it is often through collaboration that the products of cognitive surplus are born. The Hacker News community is focused around collaboration. As previously mentioned, Ask HN and Show HN posts particularly, rely on this community input. Founders can post beta-versions of their startups and be critiqued by experienced professionals.

Collaborative Tools

Luis Suarez talked to our class, remotely, about the benefits of collaborative tools. His focus on non-traditional knowledge management has taken him away from email and into a realm of more experimental technologies. That he has successfully retired from email at a corporation as large as IBM is a testament to his fortitude and devotion towards an email-less Internet.

Throughout his talk, and on his blog, Suarez places on emphasis on the importance of collaboration. Communication shouldn't be sequestered into a difficult to navigate thread that provides near-continual distraction (i.e. email).

Many HN users are founders or contractors working remotely. These users rely on effective and efficient collaborative communication tools (O'Reilly's future Internet coming to fruition). The site itself fosters much discussion on the topic.

Hacker News has about 30 editors who collaborate to keep content reasonable and on-topic. In addition to this, the platform allows the community to self-moderate. Users can upvote posts they deem constructive and on-topic. After achieving a certain threshold of these upvotes, users gain the ability to downvote comments they deem unconstructive or inflammatory. So, not only do many users rely on collaborative tools to complete their job, they rely on them to preseve the value and integrity of the site.
